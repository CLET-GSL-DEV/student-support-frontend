# The Handoff, Part Two: Shipping Complete Software

From design system to production, with standards applied where they earn their keep, and screens
that could not have been generated by a template. Same voice as the first manual, same rule: every
section is a habit whose absence I have watched produce a specific, expensive failure. The sections
follow the order of a real build. Do not reorder them; most disasters are sections done out of
sequence.

---

## 1. Understand the product before touching a tool

**Procedure.** Before any design or code, write four things in plain language and get them
confirmed. One: the product's single job, in one sentence a user would recognize ("lets a law
student register for a bar exam sitting without visiting an office"). Two: the primary user,
described by what they know and what device and network they actually have — not a persona poster, a
constraint list. Three: the three workflows that carry 90% of the value. Four: the non-functional
facts that will silently shape everything — expected load, data sensitivity, uptime tolerance,
regulatory exposure, who maintains it after you. If the client cannot answer these, that is your
first deliverable, not an obstacle to the real work. Everything downstream — every token in the
design system, every table in the schema, every standard you invoke — is derived from this page.
When a later decision feels arbitrary, it is because this page was skipped or is wrong.

**Example.** A registration portal for a government education body. The single job took a day of
questioning to pin down, and it changed the build: the client asked for "a portal," but the job was
"let an applicant complete registration in one sitting on a phone over intermittent mobile data."
That one sentence killed the multi-page wizard, mandated resumable form state saved server-side per
step, and set the performance budget before a single screen existed.

**Failure prevented.** Building a technically excellent product for a user who does not exist — the
desktop-first, always-online, patient user that lives in the developer's imagination. This failure
is invisible until launch, at which point it is total.

## 2. Build the design system before any screen

**Procedure.** The design system is not decoration applied at the end; it is the constitution the
screens must obey, and it comes first. Define it as a token system: 4–6 named colors with hex values
and _rules for use_ (which is for actions, which for danger, which never touches text); a type scale
with two or three faces assigned to roles — a characterful display face used with restraint, a
workhorse body face, a utility face for data and captions — with sizes, weights, and line-heights
fixed as tokens; a spacing scale (one base unit, multiples only); border radii, elevation, and
motion durations as named values. Then define the component inventory the product actually needs —
buttons, inputs, tables, cards, alerts, empty states, error states — each in every state (default,
hover, focus, disabled, loading, error) _before_ it appears on a screen. Every screen thereafter is
assembly, not invention. If a screen needs something the system doesn't have, the system gets
extended deliberately and the extension is documented; a screen never invents privately.

**Example.** On a multi-system programme, forty-plus applications built by different hands needed to
feel like one institution. The token file — one JSON of colors, type, and spacing consumed by every
frontend — meant a developer who had never spoken to the designer still produced screens that
matched. When the client requested a rebrand mid-delivery, it was a token change and a redeploy, not
forty renovation projects.

**Failure prevented.** Screen-by-screen drift: each page individually fine, the product collectively
incoherent, and every visual decision relitigated on every screen. Also the late-rebrand
catastrophe, which is only a catastrophe for products without a token layer.

## 3. Make it look designed, not generated

This is your explicit requirement, so it gets its own discipline. AI-assembled interfaces are
recognizable, and the tells are specific. Know them so you can refuse them.

**The tells.** Current generated design clusters around a few looks: the warm cream background with
a high-contrast serif and a terracotta accent; the near-black page with one acid-green or vermilion
highlight; the broadsheet layout of hairline rules and dense columns. Beyond palette, the deeper
tells: numbered section markers (01 / 02 / 03) on content that is not actually a sequence; a hero
that is a big number with a small label and a gradient; gradient-heavy cards with identical rounded
corners repeated forever; motion sprinkled everywhere and meaning nothing; copy that sells instead
of explains ("Empower your journey"). Any one of these can be a legitimate choice for a specific
brief. All of them together, regardless of subject, is the signature of no one having decided
anything.

**Procedure.** First, ground the design in the subject's own world. A legal-education platform has a
visual vernacular — case reporters, court typography, seals, the physical weight of statute books —
that a food-delivery app does not. Mine the subject's real materials, instruments, and language for
the palette, the type direction, the iconography. A design derived from the subject cannot look like
a design derived from a template, because the template has never met the subject. Second, choose one
signature element — the single thing this product will be remembered by: a distinctive way of
presenting a case number, a document-like texture in the records view, a characterful confirmation
moment — and spend your entire boldness budget there. Everything around the signature stays quiet,
disciplined, and precise. Third, run the generic-check: before building, ask honestly what you would
have produced for _any_ product in this category. Wherever your plan matches that default, the match
is the problem — revise that part and be able to say why the revision fits this subject. Fourth,
treat copy as design material: name things by what the user controls ("Register for the July
sitting," not "Submit application form"), keep one name for one action through the whole flow, make
errors state what happened and what to do next, never apologize vaguely. Templated copy makes a
distinctive layout look generated anyway. Finally, hold the quality floor without announcing it:
responsive to a cheap phone, visible keyboard focus, honest empty states, reduced-motion respected.
Distinctiveness on top of a broken floor is a costume.

**Example.** A student-records system whose default design would have been the universal admin
dashboard — sidebar, stat cards, blue accent. Grounded instead in the institution's own materials:
the transcript view was designed as a document, with the typographic gravity of the paper transcript
it replaced, and that single decision cascaded a type and spacing system nothing generic could
produce. Users described it as "official" — which was the entire brand requirement, achieved without
a logo in sight.

**Failure prevented.** The product that works perfectly and inspires no confidence — because it
looks like every AI demo the client has seen, and clients now recognize the look. For government and
enterprise work, looking generated reads as _being_ careless, and it undermines trust in the parts
of the system the client cannot see.

## 4. Architect in contracts you can check

**Procedure.** Before implementation, fix three artifacts. The data model: entities, relationships,
and — most neglected — ownership and lifecycle of every record (who creates it, who may change it,
what happens on deletion, what must be retained and for how long). The API contract: every
endpoint's request, response, and _error_ shapes written down before either side builds, so frontend
and backend can proceed in parallel against a shared truth instead of each other's guesses. The
boundary map: which module owns which decision, with the rule that data crosses boundaries only
through declared interfaces. Record each significant choice as a short decision record — the choice,
the alternatives, the reason — one paragraph each. The test of a good architecture is the same as in
the first manual: every piece independently checkable. If you cannot test the payment module without
standing up the whole system, the boundary is drawn wrong.

**Example.** Two teams, frontend and backend, six hundred kilometers apart. The API contract was
written, argued over, and frozen in week one — including error shapes and pagination. Integration
week, historically the graveyard of such projects, took two days, and every discrepancy found was
traceable to a documented deviation from the contract, not to a guess.

**Failure prevented.** Integration hell — components that each work and a system that doesn't — and
the archaeology problem: six months later, nobody remembers why the queue is there, so nobody dares
remove it and nobody dares rely on it.

## 5. Apply standards where the risk warrants, not as wallpaper

**Procedure.** Standards are pre-packaged answers to risks; invoke them where the risk exists, at
the depth the risk demands. The sorting: for _product quality_, use ISO/IEC 25010's characteristics
— functional suitability, performance, compatibility, usability, reliability, security,
maintainability, portability — not as a compliance stamp but as a checklist of questions, each
answered with a measurable target or an explicit "not a risk here, skipped." For _security
management_, ISO/IEC 27001's control areas tell you what a serious operation covers — access
control, cryptography, logging, incident response, supplier management — again scaled to exposure: a
public payment system gets the full treatment, an internal read-only dashboard gets the subset that
matters. For _testing_, the ISO/IEC/IEEE 29119 spirit: documented test strategy, traceability from
requirement to test, evidence retained. For _privacy_, the local data-protection statute is not
optional and is designed-in (section 8), never bolted on. For _accessibility_, WCAG 2.1 AA whenever
the public or a workforce uses it — which is nearly always. The discipline is writing, per project,
a one-page standards map: which standard, applied to what depth, evidenced how, and — equally
important — which standards were consciously _not_ applied and why. That page is what separates
engineering judgment from both negligence and theater.

**Example.** A government contract listing eight standards. The standards map allocated depth by
risk: full 27001-aligned controls and audit logging on the systems holding personal data; 25010
performance and reliability targets, with numbers, on the public-facing registration flow; WCAG
audits on everything citizens touch; and a documented decision that portability requirements were
satisfied by containerization alone for the internal tools. At acceptance review, the map itself did
more to establish credibility than any single control, because it proved the standards had been
_thought about_ rather than pasted.

**Failure prevented.** Two symmetric failures. Standards theater: a compliance matrix full of ticks
with no evidence behind them, which collapses at the first audit. And standards paralysis: applying
every clause of everything to a three-user internal tool, burning the budget that the genuinely
high-risk system needed.

## 6. Build in vertical slices on a walking skeleton

**Procedure.** The first thing built is the walking skeleton: the thinnest possible end-to-end path
— one trivial feature through real frontend, real API, real database, real authentication, deployed
through the real pipeline to a real environment. It proves the plumbing while the plumbing is cheap
to fix. Thereafter, build in vertical slices: complete workflows finished top to bottom — screen,
logic, data, tests, error handling — not horizontal layers ("all the backend first"). A slice is
done when a user could run it in production, including its failure paths; "done except error
handling" is not done, it is the dangerous kind of undone. Around the slices, the non-negotiable
hygiene: version control with reviewed pull requests, no direct pushes to main; three environments
(development, staging that mirrors production, production) with configuration in environment
variables and secrets never in the repository; database changes as versioned, reversible migrations
only; a continuous-integration gate that runs the tests on every merge.

**Example.** A ninety-day delivery. Week one produced nothing a client would applaud — a login page
and one record retrievable end to end — but it flushed out the SSL, CORS, and connection-pool
problems that traditionally surface in week eleven. From week two, every Friday demo was a genuinely
finished workflow, and the client's confidence compounded weekly instead of resting on a promised
final reveal.

**Failure prevented.** The 90%-done trap: months of horizontal progress, every layer "nearly
finished," nothing shippable, and all integration risk deferred to the end — where it is most
expensive and the deadline is closest. Also the demo-then-rewrite cycle, where what was shown was
never on the road to production.

## 7. Test by trying to break it, in writing, with evidence

**Procedure.** Acceptance criteria are written _before_ the code, per slice, in the form "given /
when / then" — they are the specification, and the tests are their executable form. Distribute
effort as a pyramid: many fast unit tests on the logic that can be wrong (calculations, validations,
state transitions — not getters), a solid band of integration tests on the seams (API contract
adherence, database behavior, third-party boundaries), a thin set of end-to-end tests on the
workflows from section 1 that carry the value. Then apply the first manual's section 4: the test
suite passing is the claim; re-derive it by a different route. Test the failure paths deliberately —
wrong input, double submission, expired session, network drop mid-payment, concurrent edits —
because users find these in week one and test plans that only rehearse success find nothing. Keep
the evidence: which tests ran, against which build, with what result, traceable to requirements.
That trail is what a 29119-style review asks for, and it is also what saves you when a client
reports as a bug something that was tested and signed off.

**Example.** A fee-payment flow, fully green in tests. The break-it pass tried the double-tap on a
slow connection — two payment requests, one click. It duplicated the transaction. An idempotency key
fixed it in an afternoon before launch; in production it would have been a reconciliation nightmare
and a headline.

**Failure prevented.** The green-suite illusion: high coverage of the happy path, zero coverage of
the paths users actually stumble into, and the discovery of the difference by the public. Also the
unfalsifiable bug dispute — without evidence of what was tested, every client-reported issue becomes
an argument instead of a lookup.

## 8. Bake in security and privacy; they do not retrofit

**Procedure.** Before building each sensitive slice, run a fifteen-minute threat model: what data
does this touch, who wants it, what are the entry points, what is the worst credible outcome. Then
the floor, which is not negotiable on anything internet-facing: the OWASP Top 10 addressed by
construction — parameterized queries only, output encoding, server-side validation of every input
regardless of client checks; authentication with proven libraries, never hand-rolled crypto or
session logic; authorization enforced on the server per request (the URL being unguessable is not
authorization); secrets in a vault or environment, rotated, and absent from the repository's entire
history; TLS everywhere; dependencies scanned and patched on a schedule, because most breaches walk
in through a library nobody was watching. For personal data, the statute's demands become design
features: collect the minimum, record the lawful basis, encrypt at rest, log access to sensitive
records in an append-only audit trail, build the data-subject operations (export, correction,
deletion) as real features — a deletion request answered by a three-week manual scramble is a
compliance failure that was actually a design failure. Write down the data-breach response path
before you need it.

**Example.** An admissions system holding minors' records. The audit-log requirement, designed in
from the schema up, cost two days. Eight months post-launch, an access complaint arrived; the
append-only trail answered in one query who had viewed the record and when, and closed the matter in
a day. The same capability retrofitted would have been weeks of work and, for historical access,
simply impossible — the data would not exist.

**Failure prevented.** The breach that ends the company's public-sector career, and its quieter
sibling: the regulator's question that cannot be answered because the evidence was never being
collected. Security retrofitted is security theater with a changelog.

## 9. Production readiness is a feature you build, not a phase you enter

**Procedure.** "It works" and "it is operable" are different claims; ship only the second.
Observability: structured logs with correlation IDs so one user's journey can be traced across
services; metrics on the four signals (traffic, errors, latency, saturation); alerts on symptoms
users feel (error rate, response time), routed to a human, tested by deliberately breaking staging
once. Deployment: scripted and repeatable, with a rehearsed rollback — a rollback never attempted is
a rumor, not a capability. Data safety: automated backups _and_ a restore actually performed to
staging on a schedule, because an untested backup is a hope. Load: the public flows tested at a
multiple of expected peak before the peak arrives on its own schedule (registration deadlines do not
negotiate). Then the handover, which for any capability-transfer engagement is a deliverable with
acceptance criteria of its own: runbooks for the five most likely incidents, an architecture
document a new engineer can onboard from in a day, credentials transferred through a proper vault,
and — the real test — the client's own team performing a deploy and a restore while you watch,
before you leave. If they cannot operate it in your presence, they cannot operate it in your
absence, and the phone calls will prove it.

**Example.** A results-publication system with a known spike: the entire user base within one hour
of an announcement. Load-tested at five times the projected peak three weeks early; the connection
pool and one unindexed query fell over in staging, quietly, on a Tuesday. On results day the system
held, and the client never knew there had been a problem — which is what operational competence
looks like from the outside.

**Failure prevented.** The launch-day collapse in front of the largest audience the product will
ever have; the un-restorable backup discovered during the disaster it was meant to survive; and the
handover that transfers a zip file instead of a capability, converting "capability transfer" from a
contract clause into a breach of one.

## 10. The mistakes that look like competence and aren't

**Demo-driven development.** The tell: what impresses on Friday is hard-coded, unauthenticated, or
single-user underneath. The fix: demo only slices that are production-done; a smaller true demo
beats a larger false one, because the false one is a debt with the client as creditor.

**Feature completeness over path completeness.** The tell: forty features, each 90% done; no
workflow 100% done. The fix: vertical slices, and a definition of done that includes the failure
paths.

**Framework fashion as architecture.** The tell: the stack chosen from this month's discourse rather
than from the client's team, the hiring market, and a ten-year maintenance horizon. The fix: boring
technology by default; spend novelty only where the problem is genuinely novel, and document why.

**The beautiful dashboard on wrong data.** The tell: polish concentrated where it is visible,
correctness assumed where it is not. Charts get design reviews; the aggregation query gets nobody.
The fix: the data pipeline is load-bearing (first manual, section 3); verify it with the effort its
invisibility demands.

**Compliance-matrix theater.** The tell: every standard ticked, no artifact behind any tick. The
fix: the standards map of section 5 — depth by risk, evidence named, omissions justified in writing.

**Premature abstraction.** The tell: plugin systems, generic engines, and configuration layers for
requirements nobody has stated, sold as foresight. The fix: build the concrete case; abstract on the
second or third real instance, when the pattern is a fact instead of a prediction.

**The heroic manual deploy.** The tell: releases that depend on one person's memory and steady
hands, narrated as dedication. The fix: if the deploy needs a hero, the deploy is broken; script it
until it is boring.

**Distinctiveness in the wrong budget.** The tell: five experimental fonts, animated everything, and
a broken tab order — boldness spent everywhere, which is the same as taste spent nowhere. The fix:
one signature element, a quiet system around it, and the accessibility floor intact underneath
(section 3).

## The self-test

Run all five on the product — per release, and per slice — before calling anything done.

1. **Can a real user, on their real device and network, complete each of the three core workflows
   end to end — including the failure paths — today?**
2. **Does every screen derive from the token system, and can I name the one signature element this
   product will be remembered by — and the subject-specific reason for it?**
3. **For each standard on this project's map: where is the evidence, at the depth the risk demands —
   and for each standard skipped, where is the written reason?**
4. **If production fails at 2 a.m., will an alert reach a human, will the logs reconstruct what
   happened, and has the rollback and the restore each been rehearsed — not planned, rehearsed?**
5. **If our team vanished tomorrow, could the client's engineers deploy, operate, and extend this
   from the handover materials alone — and have they already proven it once in front of us?**

The whole manual is one instruction wearing ten coats: a product is not the sum of its screens. It
is a set of promises — to the user, the operator, the auditor, and the maintainer who arrives after
you — and every section above is just one of those promises made checkable. Ship promises you have
tested, in a product that could only have been made for this client. Nothing generated looks like
that, because nothing generated ever met the client.

— End of manual, part two.
